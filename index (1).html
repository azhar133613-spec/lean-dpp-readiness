Lean Management Practical Project — SEU, 3rd Year

A Lean toolkit for Digital Product Passport readiness in Bangladesh's RMG factories.

The EU's Digital Product Passport is coming for textiles around 2027–2028. Most SME garment factories already hold the data it needs — scattered across cutting tickets, supplier PDFs, and Excel sheets. This framework uses 5S, VSM, 5 Whys, Kaizen, and Kanban to find it, link it, and keep it.

01 — CONTEXT
Why now

The DPP clock has already started

Based on currently published EU sources, here is the indicative timeline for textiles. Dates have slipped before — but the direction is firm.

2024
ESPR enters into force (18 July 2024) — the legal framework behind the DPP.
2025
Ecodesign Working Plan 2025–2030 confirms textiles as a priority category.
2026
EU Central DPP Registry expected live (19 July). Unsold-goods destruction ban begins for large firms.
● we are here
2027
Textile-specific delegated act expected, starting an ~18-month transition window.
2028
Mandatory DPP compliance widely expected to begin — SMEs typically get longer deadlines.
Honesty note: as of mid-2026 the textile delegated act has not yet been published. This framework treats "readiness" as forward-looking preparation — better data and documentation habits — not a claim that a legal deadline is imminent today.
02 — TOOLKIT
Methodology

Five Lean tools, retasked for data

Each tool keeps its original Lean job — but the "workplace" being organised is the factory's data and documentation, not just its floor.

TOOL 01

5S

Sort, Set in order, Shine, Standardise, Sustain — the classic workplace organisation method.

Applied to DPPSort which material/supplier records exist, standardise naming & formats, sustain update habits — the foundation of any DPP data feed.
TOOL 02

Value Stream Mapping

Maps material and information flow end-to-end to expose waste.

Applied to DPPMaps the information flow for one product line — fabric to export — flagging exactly where origin and composition data is captured or lost.
TOOL 03

5 Whys

Root-cause analysis by repeatedly asking "why" a problem occurs.

Applied to DPPDrills into each scorecard gap — e.g. "fabric composition isn't recorded per batch" — until the real, fixable root cause appears.
TOOL 04

Kaizen

Continuous, incremental improvement involving everyone.

Applied to DPPTurns scorecard gaps into a 30/60/90-day roadmap of small, low-cost steps — templates and checklists, not new software.
TOOL 05

Kanban

Visual pull-system for managing work in progress.

Applied to DPPA simple To Do / In Progress / Done board tracks the roadmap's documentation tasks across departments.

04 — CASE STUDY
Illustrative · hypothetical

"Green Stitch Apparels Ltd." — where does the data disappear?

A fictional ~450-worker knitwear SME near Gazipur, built to be representative of typical BGMEA SME members. Tracing one order across the floor shows fabric-level data exists at the start — and vanishes before packing.

Fabric Supplier

✓ exists

Composition + GRS certificate sent as a PDF — but not linked digitally to the order.

Receiving / Inspection

✗ missing

Fabric inspection report logged on paper only — not digitised.

Cutting

~ partial

Cutting ticket references the fabric roll number.

Sewing

✗ missing

No material data carried over — line tracks output quantity only.

Finishing & QC

✗ missing

QC checks quality, not composition or origin.

Packing & Export

~ order-level

Packing list + invoice exist, but only at order level.

5 Whys — chasing the root cause

1
Why?

The cutting ticket records the fabric roll number, but it isn't carried onto sewing or packing documents.

2
Why?

Sewing and finishing documents were designed only to track output quantity, not material origin.

3
Why?

They were designed years ago for productivity tracking — before traceability was a buyer requirement.

4
Why?

No department owns cross-stage data continuity — each one optimises its own paperwork in isolation.

5
Root cause

There's no standardised "order data card" that travels with the order through every stage — a 5S "standardise" gap at the document level.

05 — ROADMAP
Kaizen plan

30 / 60 / 90 — low-cost, no new software

The root cause above doesn't need an ERP system. It needs one standardised document that travels with the order.

DAYS 1 – 30
  • Pilot a one-page "Order Traceability Card" — fabric roll #, composition %, supplier, certificate ref — that travels with each cutting batch.5S (standardise) + Kaizen
  • Create one shared folder for supplier certificates (OCS / GRS / OEKO-TEX), named by supplier + expiry date.5S (sort & set in order)
DAYS 31 – 60
  • Train sewing & finishing supervisors to record the Traceability Card # on existing production sheets.Kaizen — staff training
  • Re-run the Readiness Scorecard for a follow-up score vs. baseline.PDCA cycle
DAYS 61 – 90
  • Pilot a shared spreadsheet linking Order # → Fabric Roll # → Composition → Certificate ref → Shipment #.Digital 5S + Kanban
  • Review results with management; decide on extending to a second product line ahead of the 2027 delegated act.Kaizen review

06 — TAKEAWAYS
Conclusion

Start with documentation, not software

  • 01

    The biggest readiness gains for SMEs come from standardising existing paper/Excel records — not buying ERP platforms.

  • 02

    Treat DPP readiness as a Kaizen journey: small, continuous improvements compound over the ~18–24 months before mandatory compliance is expected.

  • 03

    Engage buying houses early — many EU brands request transparency data ahead of legal deadlines.

  • 04

    BGMEA, BUFT and NITER are likely future channels for Bangladesh-specific DPP guidance.
